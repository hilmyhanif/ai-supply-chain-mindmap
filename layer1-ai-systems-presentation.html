01 — Definition

What Is the Physical Industrial Base?

The physical/industrial base of AI refers to the hardware infrastructure and manufacturing industries that form the foundation of all AI systems. This layer encompasses semiconductor chips, the equipment and materials to produce them, and the facilities (fabs, labs, factories) that build and assemble computing devices.

Modern AI relies on integrated circuits packed with billions of nanometer-scale transistors, fabricated on silicon wafers using advanced photolithography—etching extremely fine circuit patterns via ultraviolet lasers and chemical processing. Since early microprocessors in the 1970s, the industry has followed Moore's Law, relentlessly improving chip density and performance to power today's AI accelerators.

⚙️

A Global Supply Chain

Designing and mass-producing advanced chips is enormously complex — no single country or company can do it all. The chain spans raw material extraction, wafer fabrication, chip packaging, and final device assembly, involving dozens of specialized players worldwide.

🔬

EUV Lithography as a Chokepoint

Extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines are essential for chips at 7nm and below. Netherlands-based ASML is the sole global supplier of these systems — making it one of the most strategically critical companies in the entire AI stack.

🏗️

Wafer Fabs & Assembly

Chips are fabricated in ultra-clean, capital-intensive foundries. Once wafers are cut into dies, they undergo assembly, packaging, and testing before integration into servers, smartphones, or IoT devices.

🖥️

Supercomputers & AI Servers

In practice, AI hardware is deployed in racks of CPU/GPU/TPU cards with massive parallelism and fast interconnects. Cutting-edge supercomputers aggregate thousands of processors, enabling training of large AI models at unprecedented speeds.

Leading nations share common success factors: sustained R&D investment (billions annually), clustered ecosystems (e.g., Taiwan's Hsinchu Science Park linking universities, ITRI, and industry since the 1980s), and strategic government policy (South Korea's memory chip rise; the U.S. CHIPS Act for onshore fab capacity). Recent export controls — including U.S. curbs on top AI chips to China — underscore how strategically critical these assets have become.

03 — Indonesia's Position

Indonesia: Current Standing & Strategic Potential

Indonesia is currently a small but growing participant in the semiconductor hardware arena. Its activity has so far been concentrated in the back-end of the supply chain — assembly, testing, and packaging — rather than high-end chip fabrication or design.

Companies like Germany's Infineon operate assembly and test facilities in Batam, leveraging Indonesia's competitive labor costs and strategic Southeast Asian location. This foothold in packaging and assembly represents an important link in the chain — though generally lower value-add than design or fabrication.

Indonesia's natural resource endowments and large demographics offer meaningful upside. Rather than exporting raw materials, there is a significant opportunity to develop local downstream processing — such as converting silica into wafer stock — to capture more value domestically.

The government has signaled ambition: it has formed a Semiconductor Task Force and committed an initial $125 million in partnership with ARM Holdings (UK) to develop a national chip design ecosystem, training Indonesian engineers on ARM architecture.

04 — Ecosystem Roadmap

Building Indonesia's AI Hardware Ecosystem

Becoming a significant player in the global AI hardware supply chain requires concerted action across four pillars:

01
Policy

National Strategy & Regulatory Clarity

Formulate a clear national semiconductor strategy with defined targets — production milestones, priority segments (AI chips, automotive MCUs), and export objectives. Streamline regulations: tax incentives for fabs and design centers, simplified import/export procedures for high-tech equipment, and robust IP protection to reassure investors. Malaysia's national electronics plan and India's semiconductor mission offer actionable reference models.

02
Talent

Human Capital Development

Strengthen university and vocational curricula in semiconductor physics, microelectronics, and AI hardware engineering. Establish partnerships with leading institutions in the US, Taiwan, and India for knowledge transfer. Offer government scholarships for specialization in chip design and fabrication — with commitments to return and build local industry. Address brain drain by creating competitive career paths and research opportunities domestically.

03
Infrastructure

Industrial Infrastructure & Manufacturing Zones

Upgrade industrial parks with cleanroom facilities, stable power, ultra-pure water supply, and efficient logistics. Consider a dedicated "chip manufacturing zone" with streamlined customs. Invest in upstream capabilities — local wafer slicing, polishing, and packaging substrate production from domestic silica. Court targeted foreign investment: invite foundries for mature-node fabs (28nm–45nm), and expand OSAT operations through public-private partnerships.

04
R&D

Research, Innovation & International Collaboration

Establish a centralized Semiconductor Research Center bringing together government labs, universities, and industry. Focus on niche areas aligned with Indonesia's context — AI chips optimized for local languages, or power-efficient designs for tropical environments. Expand the ARM partnership model to include South Korea, Japan, and the US. Position Indonesia within ASEAN semiconductor consortia and Quad/Chip4 supply chain security alliances to accelerate the learning curve through mentorship and technology transfer.
